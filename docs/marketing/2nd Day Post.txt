Yesterday I shared how @Lian Sapan changed how I think about job searching.

Today - what I actually did with that thinking.

She showed me that most opportunities never make it to a job board. 
Companies hire when something changes - a funding round, a new VP, a product launch.
These are public signals. They're everywhere. I just wasn't looking.

I won't walk you through her full framework here - it's her work, and she teaches it far better than I could summarize.

But I'll tell you what shifted: I stopped applying and started researching.

I found websites that track startup funding in real time. Every new round was a hiring signal hiding in plain sight.

I used AI to dig into those companies - what they're building, what problems they're solving, what gaps their team might have.

Then I'd check how my experience mapped to what they actually needed. Not what a job description said. What the company's situation demanded.

I found the people who'd actually make the hiring decision. Not HR. Not a generic inbox. The person who'd feel the pain of an empty seat.

And I learned to write messages that didn't sound like everyone else's. No templates. No "I'd love to connect." Something specific enough that they knew
I'd done the work.

Each one took time. Real attention. But the responses were immediate.

More replies in two weeks than in the previous months.

The method works. But it doesn't scale.

And I'm an engineer.

So I started building a system that carries this logic automatically - 7 AI agents that analyze the resume, find the signals, research the companies, identify the right people,
and draft outreach that sounds like a human did the homework.

Over the next few posts I'll show the actual output. What it finds, what it writes, and where it still gets things wrong.
