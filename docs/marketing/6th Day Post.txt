I built an AI that writes outreach messages.

Then I built a kill switch into it.

Nothing sends without my approval. Not one word.

Here's why.

I tested the outreach draft for a VP of Engineering at a fintech startup. The AI pulled from my candidate profile, the company dossier, even a recent podcast the VP appeared on.

The draft was good. Referenced specific technical challenges. Mentioned their Series B. Connected my distributed systems work to their scaling problems.

But it called their product "a payments platform."

They're a lending platform.

One word. Would have killed the whole message.

It nailed the research. Then got the one thing wrong that actually mattered.

So here's how it works.

The system drafts the message, shows me why it chose each detail, and waits. I edit, rewrite, or trash it. Then — and only then — I hit send.

I didn't want to build a faster version of "I noticed your impressive background and thought we'd be a great fit." Sent to 400 people. Personalized with nothing.

AI drafts. Humans decide.

That's not a limitation. That's the whole point.

The person reading your message can tell the difference between something crafted and something blasted. Even when they can't explain how.
